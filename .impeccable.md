## Design Context

### Users
Family and close friends of Mika, a 14-year-old baker in Israel. The audience is Hebrew-speaking, RTL layout. They browse the app to find Mika's personal, handcrafted recipes — desserts, pastries, and home cooking. Context: casual browsing at home, often on mobile. The feeling should be personal, like flipping through someone's curated collection, not a food delivery service.

### Brand Personality
Three words: **handcrafted, colorful, alive**

Mika is a teenager who bakes with passion. The app should feel like her kitchen — energetic, a little messy in the best way, full of color and warmth. Not a professional cookbook. Not corporate. Not your grandmother's recipe box. More like a zine made by someone who genuinely loves what they're doing.

Emotional goals: excitement to cook, pride in the recipes, warmth and belonging for the family audience.

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Theme**: Light (family browsing during the day, cheerful context)
- **Energy**: High — bold colors, clear hierarchy, confident type choices
- **Anti-references**: Corporate cookbooks (Bon Appétit web), generic food delivery apps (Wolt/Deliveroo style), Pinterest mood-board overload, grandmother's recipe box (beige + brown + Times New Roman)
- **References feel**: A young chef's personal zine; a colorful sticker book; a market stand with hand-painted signs
- **Color direction**: Warm cream base is right. But the accent palette needs real color contrast — actual yellow, actual coral/orange, real green — not the current all-pink-rose pastels masquerading as different colors
- **Background**: Cream (`#fdf2f0`) — keep it. It's warm and right.

### Design Principles
1. **Be Mika, not a template** — every design decision should feel personal and specific to a 14-year-old baker, not generic food-app aesthetics
2. **Color does the work** — use real, saturated accent colors (not pastels of the same hue) to create excitement and category identity
3. **Consistency as fluency** — the app should speak the same visual language on every page (same card treatment, same button styles, same spacing rhythm)
4. **Ingredients deserve respect** — recipe content pages should feel warm and readable, not clinical. Frank Ruhl Libre for Hebrew body text is the right instinct.
5. **Mobile-first delight** — most family browsing will be on phone; the experience there should be just as rich as desktop
